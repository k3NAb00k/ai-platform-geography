# Introduction

Artificial intelligence is increasingly framed as a cornerstone of economic growth, national competitiveness, and technological leadership. As AI industries expand, the geographical distribution of AI jobs carries important implications for regional inequality, urban development, and access to high-skill employment. AI labor markets are often assumed to be geographically concentrated in major urban technology hubs, like Silicon Valley in the United States. In India, cities such as Bengaluru, Hyderabad, and Pune are widely recognized as centers of software development and AI innovation. Urban economic theory suggests that high-skill industries cluster spatially because firms benefit from knowledge spillovers, dense labor markets, and shared infrastructure. However, the rise of digital labor platforms such as global talent intermediaries and remote hiring marketplaces may be altering how AI job opportunities are distributed and made visible. If AI employment continues to cluster in a small number of metropolitan centers, it may deepen spatial inequalities, concentrating opportunity, income, and innovation capacity in already advanced regions.

This project investigates whether platform intermediation reshapes the observed geography of AI job posting in India. Using a combined dataset of 2,129 AI-related job postings scraped from open online sources, I compare the geographic concentration of postings from traditional employers with those mediated by digital talent platforms. Specifically, I ask whether platform-based job postings are more geographically dispersed than non-platform postings, and whether they reduce the apparent dominance of major hubs such as Bengaluru. If platform-mediated hiring disperses job posting across a wider range of cities, it could signal a partial decoupling of AI labor markets from traditional tech hubs. Conversely, if platform postings replicate existing geographic concentration, digital intermediation may reinforce rather than disrupt urban clustering. Understanding whether digital platforms redistribute or reinforce geographic concentration is critical for policymakers, firms, and workers navigating the future of AI-driven economic transformation. By examining employer concentration, geographic clustering, and distributional structure, this study contributes to broader debates about urban agglomeration, digital intermediation, and the changing spatial organization of high-skill work.
